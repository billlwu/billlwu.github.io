def get_vol(s, logr):
    '''
    Compute weighted volatility using using a running sum
    s: decay factor
    '''
    var = 0.0
    lam = 0.0
    var_list = []
    for r in logr:
        lam = lam*s + 1
        var = (1-1.0/lam)*var + (1.0/lam)*r**2
        var_list.append(var)
    return [np.sqrt(x) for x in var_list]

We describe how the running sum advances:

At time i (first line are the returns assuming 0 mean, followed by their weights),

. . . $r_3^{2}$ $r_2^{2}$ $r_1^{2}$

. . . $s^2$ $s$ $1$

At time i+1,

. . . $r_3^{2}$ $r_2^{2}$ $r_1^{2}$ $r_0^{2}$

. . . $s^3$ $s^2$ $s$ $1$

Assuming infinite terms then all the coefficients sum to $\frac{1}{1-s}$. We can show that $V = sV_i + (1-s)r_0^2$. Function get_vol carries out this procedure.

Iter 1, the running sum is $r_0^2$

Iter 2, the running sum is $\frac{s}{1+s}V_{old} + \frac{1}{1+s}r_1^2$

Iter 3, the running sum is $\frac{s+s^2}{1+s+s^2}V_{old} + \frac{1}{1+s+s^2}r_2^2$

Having a small (close to 0) $s$ means less weight is given to the previous running sum, (i.e. retaining less memory or faster decay) and vice versa.
